## Why This Exists

The Book of Common Prayer has been forming Christians in the English language for nearly five hundred years. The texts are public domain. The tradition is living. The infrastructure for accessing it digitally — structured, citable, version-controlled, agent-readable — was not.

This is an attempt to build that infrastructure quietly and get it right.

Not for profit. Not for platform. As a service to the community, in the same spirit Charles Wohlers began his work in 1996.

## The Book

In 1549, Thomas Cranmer gave the English-speaking world a single book for common prayer. It was a political act, a theological act, and — whatever its authors intended — a literary act of the first order. The cadences Cranmer forged from Sarum Use, the Latin fathers, Lutheran reforms, and his own ear for English prose produced a document that has never gone out of use.

Outside of Shakespeare, no English literary work has achieved comparable diffusion — prayed in cathedrals and bush chapels, in colonial outposts and royal households, in the holds of ships and the parlors of the dying. The Book of Common Prayer has been shaping the way English-speaking people address God, mark their dead, and marry their children for nearly five hundred years. Its phrases have passed so thoroughly into the bloodstream of the language that most people who use them have no idea where they came from.

The American editions are their own chapter. The 1789 revision — produced by a young church in a young republic, navigating the strange territory of Anglican practice without a crown — was an act of both continuity and independence. The 1892 and 1928 revisions deepened that American voice while holding the thread back to Cranmer. These are not merely ecclesiastical documents. They are primary sources for the cultural, social, and literary history of the United States — records of how a people prayed, what they believed about death and marriage and sickness and nationhood, and how those beliefs shifted across two centuries.

They deserve careful preservation. Not as museum pieces. As living documents that still work.

That is what this repository is for.
